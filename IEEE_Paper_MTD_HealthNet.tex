\begin{abstract}
Hospital Local Area Networks (LANs) rely predominantly on static IP configurations, creating predictable attack surfaces that adversaries exploit through reconnaissance, lateral movement, and ransomware deployment. This paper presents \textbf{MTD-HealthNet}, a Software-Defined Networking (SDN) based Moving Target Defense framework that employs zone-based dynamic IP hopping and NAT randomization to continuously mutate the externally visible network topology of hospital environments. The system architecture comprises a Ryu OpenFlow 1.3 controller that orchestrates periodic IP rotation across three risk-stratified hospital zones---Critical, Imaging, and Administrative---with configurable hopping intervals of 120s, 100s, and 80s respectively. Internal hosts retain stable private addresses (10.0.0.0/24) while their public-facing identities (172.16.0.0/16) are continuously randomized, ensuring service continuity through transparent NAT translation and synchronized flow rule updates. Experimental evaluation on a Mininet-based testbed with six hospital endpoint emulators demonstrates that MTD-HealthNet reduces reconnaissance accuracy by \textbf{83.3\%} (from 6/6 to 1/6 discoverable hosts per scan cycle), increases attacker effort by a factor of \textbf{4.7$\times$}, and maintains a packet delivery ratio above \textbf{97.8\%} during IP transitions, with flow rule update latency averaging \textbf{12.4~ms}. The framework achieves a security-performance trade-off suitable for healthcare environments where both resilience and uptime are critical.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern healthcare institutions depend on interconnected digital infrastructure including Electronic Health Record (EHR) systems, patient monitoring devices, PACS imaging servers, and administrative workstations. These systems communicate over hospital Local Area Networks (LANs) that are increasingly targeted by sophisticated cyber threats. According to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, healthcare data breaches affected over 133 million records in 2023 alone, with ransomware accounting for a significant proportion of incidents.

A fundamental vulnerability in hospital networks is their reliance on \textit{static network configurations}. IP addresses, port assignments, and routing structures remain unchanged for extended periods, enabling attackers to perform reconnaissance once and retain a reliable blueprint of the network indefinitely. This predictability facilitates targeted attacks including lateral movement across network segments, IP spoofing to impersonate trusted devices, and precision ransomware deployment against critical infrastructure.

Traditional defensive mechanisms---firewalls, intrusion detection systems (IDS), and network segmentation---operate as \textit{static barriers} that do not alter the underlying attack surface. Moving Target Defense (MTD) represents a paradigm shift by proactively modifying system parameters to invalidate attacker knowledge and increase the cost of successful exploitation~\cite{lei2018survey, cai2016mtd}.

This paper presents \textbf{MTD-HealthNet}, an SDN-based MTD framework specifically designed for hospital LANs, designed and implemented by Rushyendra et al. The key contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Zone-based IP rotation architecture}: A three-tier risk classification (Critical, Imaging, Administrative) with differentiated hopping intervals that balance security strength against communication stability requirements unique to healthcare environments.
    \item \textbf{Transparent NAT randomization}: A dual-layer addressing scheme where internal private IPs remain stable while externally visible public IPs are continuously rotated via controller-managed DNAT/SNAT rules, ensuring zero-disruption to application-layer sessions.
    \item \textbf{Practical SDN implementation}: A fully functional prototype using Ryu, Mininet, and Open vSwitch with OpenFlow 1.3, validated through application-level secure transfers rather than solely ICMP-based connectivity tests.
    \item \textbf{Quantitative security evaluation}: Empirical measurement of reconnaissance degradation, attack effort amplification, and network performance overhead under dynamic IP hopping conditions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Moving Target Defense Techniques}

MTD techniques dynamically alter system configurations to create uncertainty for attackers. Lei et al.~\cite{lei2018survey} provide a comprehensive taxonomy of MTD approaches, categorizing them into network-based, host-based, and application-based techniques. Network-based MTD, which modifies IP addresses, ports, or routing paths, is particularly effective against reconnaissance-dependent attacks.

Cai et al.~\cite{cai2016mtd} survey the state of the art in MTD, identifying key characteristics including attack surface diversity, randomization entropy, and reconfiguration overhead. They highlight the trade-off between mutation frequency and system stability as an open research challenge.

\subsection{IP Hopping in SDN Environments}

Dishington et al.~\cite{dishington2019ipmux} propose IP multiplexing as an MTD technique in SDN environments, demonstrating that OpenFlow-based IP randomization can reduce scanning effectiveness while maintaining acceptable throughput. Their work validates SDN as a suitable platform for MTD deployment but does not address zone-differentiated rotation or healthcare-specific constraints.

\.{Z}al et al.~\cite{zal2024lossless} present a lossless moving target defense mechanism that minimizes packet loss during IP transitions. Their approach uses pre-computed flow rules and shadow periods to ensure continuity, a strategy that informs our flow management design.

\subsection{MTD for Healthcare and Legacy Systems}

Huang et al.~\cite{huang2021legacy} specifically address MTD deployment in legacy infrastructure environments, proposing mechanisms that accommodate devices unable to dynamically update their network configurations. This is directly relevant to hospital settings where legacy medical equipment (e.g., DICOM imaging systems, HL7 interfaces) requires fixed internal addressing. Their work motivates our dual-layer NAT approach that preserves internal stability while randomizing external visibility.

\subsection{Research Gaps}

Based on the literature analysis conducted by Siddardha and Jeeshitha, several gaps remain in the context of hospital network security:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Existing MTD frameworks rarely differentiate rotation policies by device criticality or zone sensitivity, treating all hosts uniformly.
    \item Few implementations validate communication continuity through application-layer testing under dynamic IP conditions; most rely on ICMP-only verification.
    \item The integration of policy-based access control with dynamic IP rotation in healthcare environments remains underexplored.
    \item Limited work exists on quantifying the security-performance trade-off specifically for hospital LAN topologies with heterogeneous device types.
\end{enumerate}

MTD-HealthNet addresses these gaps through zone-differentiated rotation, application-layer validation, and integrated policy enforcement within a healthcare-specific SDN deployment.

\section{System Architecture}

MTD-HealthNet employs a three-layer architecture, designed by Rushyendra et al., around the constraints and requirements of hospital network environments.

\subsection{Network Model}

The hospital LAN is modeled as a set of network zones reflecting typical healthcare segmentation practices:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Critical Zone} (hosts $h_1$, $h_2$): ICU monitoring systems and EHR servers requiring highest availability and security.
    \item \textbf{Imaging Zone} (hosts $h_3$, $h_4$): PACS servers and DICOM imaging workstations with moderate data sensitivity.
    \item \textbf{Administrative Zone} (hosts $h_5$, $h_6$): Staff workstations and administrative systems with standard security requirements.
\end{itemize}

Each host is assigned a \textit{static private IP} from the 10.0.0.0/24 subnet that never changes, and a \textit{dynamic public IP} from the 172.16.0.50--99 pool that rotates at zone-specific intervals. This dual-addressing scheme ensures that internal application configurations remain stable while the externally observable network topology is continuously mutated.

\subsection{Control Plane: Ryu SDN Controller}

The control plane, implemented by Rushyendra, is a custom Ryu SDN controller application using OpenFlow 1.3. The controller serves as the central intelligence of the MTD system, managing:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DHCP simulation}: Hosts boot with 0.0.0.0 and request IPs via simulated DHCP discovery, ensuring dynamic address assignment from system startup.
    \item \textbf{NAT management}: Bidirectional DNAT/SNAT rules translate between private and public addresses. Outbound traffic has its source IP rewritten from private to public; inbound traffic has its destination IP rewritten from public to private.
    \item \textbf{Flow rule orchestration}: OpenFlow rules are installed with a priority hierarchy (ARP: 1000, ICMP: 500, NAT: 100, default: 1) to ensure correct packet handling during transitions.
    \item \textbf{DNS resolution}: Dynamic DNS records map hostnames to current public IPs, enabling hosts to resolve each other's addresses after rotation.
    \item \textbf{Policy enforcement}: Zone-based access control lists (ACLs) are evaluated before flow installation, implementing the principle of least privilege across hospital zones.
    \item \textbf{State persistence}: All NAT mappings, DNS records, and shuffle history are stored in an SQLite database for audit trail and crash recovery.
\end{itemize}

The controller exposes a REST API on port 8000 for external monitoring and manual shuffle triggers, supporting endpoints for status queries, log retrieval, DNS resolution, and secure data transfer simulation.

\subsection{Data Plane: Mininet Topology}

The data plane, developed by Kishan, consists of a Mininet-based virtual network with six hosts connected through a central Open vSwitch (OVS). Each host runs a lightweight Python HTTP agent on port 8080, also implemented by Kishan, for receiving secure transfers and generating acknowledgment logs. A topology agent on port 8888 enables remote command execution on virtual hosts for testing and verification.

\subsection{MTD Engine: Zone Scheduler}

The zone scheduler, co-developed by Rushyendra and Kishan, is a standalone module that orchestrates IP rotation according to zone-specific policies defined in a YAML configuration file (\texttt{policies.yml}). The scheduler operates independently, communicating with the controller via REST API calls to trigger shuffles at predefined intervals.

The ACL rules, designed by Siddardha, follow a hierarchical model: Critical zone hosts can communicate with all zones, Imaging zone hosts can access Low-security zones, and Administrative zone hosts have restricted inter-zone access. This policy model reflects realistic hospital network segmentation requirements.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{IP Hopping Mechanism}

The dynamic IP hopping procedure, designed and implemented by Rushyendra, is formally described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:iphopping}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Zone-Based IP Hopping Procedure}
\label{alg:iphopping}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Zone configuration $Z = \{z_1, z_2, z_3\}$, IP pool $P$, interval map $I$
\Ensure Updated NAT mappings and flow rules
\For{each zone $z_i \in Z$}
    \If{$elapsed\_time(z_i) \geq I(z_i)$}
        \For{each host $h_j \in z_i$}
            \State $ip_{old} \gets \text{NAT}[h_j.\text{private\_ip}]$
            \State $ip_{new} \gets \text{RandomSelect}(P \setminus \text{ActiveIPs})$
            \State \text{DeleteFlows}($h_j$, $ip_{old}$)
            \State \text{InstallNATFlows}($h_j$, $ip_{new}$)
            \State \text{NAT}[$h_j.\text{private\_ip}$] $\gets ip_{new}$
            \State \text{UpdateDNS}($h_j.\text{hostname}$, $ip_{new}$)
            \State \text{LogShuffle}($h_j$, $ip_{old}$, $ip_{new}$)
        \EndFor
        \State \text{ResetTimer}($z_i$)
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The flow installation follows a specific pattern for NAT translation:

\begin{verbatim}
# Outbound: private_src -> public_src
match = OFPMatch(eth_type=0x0800,
                 ipv4_src=private_ip)
actions = [OFPActionSetField(
               ipv4_src=public_ip),
           OFPActionOutput(out_port)]

# Inbound: public_dst -> private_dst
match = OFPMatch(eth_type=0x0800,
                 ipv4_dst=public_ip)
actions = [OFPActionSetField(
               ipv4_dst=private_ip),
           OFPActionOutput(in_port)]
\end{verbatim}

Old flow rules are explicitly deleted before new ones are installed to prevent routing conflicts. The controller, as optimized by Rushyendra and Kishan, employs a brief \textit{shadow period} where both old and new rules coexist to prevent packet drops during transition.

\subsection{Threat Model}

The threat model, formulated by Siddardha et al., considers an adversary with the following capabilities:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Network reconnaissance}: The attacker can perform active scanning (e.g., Nmap) to discover hosts, open ports, and running services on the hospital LAN.
    \item \textbf{Lateral movement}: After compromising an initial foothold, the attacker attempts to traverse network segments to reach high-value targets such as EHR servers.
    \item \textbf{IP spoofing}: The attacker may forge source IP addresses to bypass access control mechanisms and impersonate trusted devices.
    \item \textbf{Ransomware deployment}: With sufficient network knowledge, the attacker targets critical infrastructure for encryption-based extortion.
\end{itemize}

The attacker is assumed to have no persistent access to the SDN controller or the internal private address space. The MTD defense aims to degrade the attacker's reconnaissance accuracy over time, increase the cost of lateral movement, and reduce the window of vulnerability for each host.

\subsection{Security Metrics}

Rushyendra and Siddardha defined the following quantitative metrics for evaluation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reconnaissance Accuracy ($R_A$)}: The fraction of hosts correctly identified by an attacker's scan relative to total active hosts, measured as:
    \begin{equation}
        R_A = \frac{|\text{Hosts discovered with valid IPs}|}{|\text{Total active hosts}|}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Attack Effort Multiplier ($E_M$)}: The ratio of scanning effort required under MTD versus a static network:
    \begin{equation}
        E_M = \frac{\text{Scans required under MTD}}{\text{Scans required under static}} = \frac{T_{hop}^{-1} \cdot T_{scan}}{1}
    \end{equation}
    where $T_{hop}$ is the hopping interval and $T_{scan}$ is the time for a complete network scan.

    \item \textbf{Packet Delivery Ratio ($PDR$)}: The ratio of successfully delivered packets to total transmitted packets during IP rotation events:
    \begin{equation}
        PDR = \frac{\text{Packets received}}{\text{Packets sent}} \times 100\%
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Flow Update Latency ($\delta_f$)}: The time elapsed between initiating an IP hop and completing flow rule installation on the data plane.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Implementation}

The system was implemented primarily by Rushyendra and Kishan using the following technology stack:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Controller}: Python 3.8 with Ryu SDN framework, eventlet 0.30.2, and OpenFlow 1.3 protocol support, containerized in Docker for reproducibility.
    \item \textbf{Network emulation}: Mininet 2.3.0 with Open vSwitch 2.13 for virtual hospital LAN topology.
    \item \textbf{Host agents}: Lightweight Python HTTP servers on each virtual host (port 8080) for secure transfer verification and acknowledgment logging.
    \item \textbf{Zone scheduler}: Standalone Python module communicating with the controller via REST API to trigger zone-specific IP rotations.
    \item \textbf{Monitoring}: Web-based dashboard (HTML/CSS/JavaScript) with AJAX polling to \texttt{/status} and \texttt{/logs} endpoints for real-time NAT binding visualization and IP rotation history.
    \item \textbf{Security validation}: Nmap for reconnaissance simulation, Wireshark for packet capture analysis, and cURL for REST API-based secure transfer testing.
\end{itemize}

The controller maintains thread-safe access to shared state (host maps, NAT tables) using mutex locks and persists all state changes to an SQLite database with schema tables for \texttt{hosts}, \texttt{nat\_mappings}, \texttt{shuffle\_log}, and \texttt{dns\_records}.

Communication validation, carried out by Kishan and Jeeshitha, was performed at the application layer using the \texttt{/sim/secure\_transfer} REST endpoint rather than relying solely on ICMP-based \texttt{pingall} tests, as NAT translation and dynamic IP hopping can cause ICMP responses to be inconsistent even when actual data transfers succeed.

\subsection{Test Scenarios}

Three primary attack scenarios, designed by Rushyendra et al., were evaluated:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Network reconnaissance}: Simulated Nmap host discovery scans (\texttt{nmap -sn 172.16.0.0/24}) executed at regular intervals to measure how IP rotation degrades scanning accuracy over time.
    \item \textbf{Lateral movement}: Attempted sequential host compromise from an Administrative zone host toward Critical zone targets, measuring the success rate of reaching intended destinations when IPs change between attack stages.
    \item \textbf{Communication stability}: Continuous REST-based secure transfers between host pairs during active IP rotation to measure packet delivery ratio and application-layer reliability.
\end{enumerate}

\textit{Assumptions}: (i) The attacker does not have access to the SDN controller or the internal 10.0.0.0/24 address space. (ii) Scan durations are comparable to hopping intervals. (iii) The Mininet environment faithfully represents the forwarding behavior of a physical SDN deployment. (iv) Performance measurements on a single-switch topology represent a lower bound on latency for multi-switch deployments.

\section{Results and Evaluation}

\subsection{Reconnaissance Degradation}

Security testing was conducted by Rushyendra and Kishan using Nmap-based scanning. In the static baseline network, an Nmap host discovery scan consistently identified all 6 hosts with 100\% accuracy across repeated scans. Under MTD-HealthNet, scanning effectiveness degraded significantly as IP addresses rotated between scan cycles.

Over 20 scan cycles (each scan taking approximately 15--20 seconds), the attacker's ability to maintain a consistent host map deteriorated as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scan cycle 1}: 6/6 hosts discovered (scan occurs before first rotation).
    \item \textbf{Scan cycles 2--5}: Average 3.2/6 hosts discovered per scan as Administrative zone IPs begin rotating.
    \item \textbf{Scan cycles 6--20}: Average 1.0/6 hosts consistently identifiable as all zones have undergone multiple rotations.
\end{itemize}

The steady-state reconnaissance accuracy converged to approximately \textbf{16.7\%} (1/6), representing an \textbf{83.3\% reduction} in attacker visibility. The residual discoverable host corresponds to the brief window during which a newly assigned IP has not yet rotated.

\subsection{Attack Effort Amplification}

Under a static network, an attacker requires a single complete scan to map all hosts. With MTD-HealthNet, the attacker must rescan after each rotation interval to maintain current knowledge. Given the minimum hopping interval of 80 seconds (Administrative zone) and an average scan duration of 18 seconds:

\begin{equation}
    E_M = \frac{80}{18} \approx 4.4\times \text{ (Administrative zone)}
\end{equation}

For the Critical zone with 120-second intervals:

\begin{equation}
    E_M = \frac{120}{18} \approx 6.7\times \text{ (Critical zone)}
\end{equation}

The weighted average across all zones yields an attack effort multiplier of \textbf{4.7$\times$}, meaning the attacker must expend approximately 4.7 times the scanning effort compared to a static network to maintain equivalent reconnaissance accuracy.

\subsection{Lateral Movement Disruption}

Kishan conducted simulated lateral movement attacks from the Administrative zone toward the Critical zone were attempted in 15 trials. Each trial involved a three-stage attack: (1) scan to identify target, (2) wait for an opportunity window, (3) attempt connection to the identified IP.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static baseline}: 15/15 successful connections (100\%).
    \item \textbf{MTD-HealthNet}: 3/15 successful connections (\textbf{20\%}).
\end{itemize}

The 80\% failure rate under MTD conditions resulted from target IPs changing between the reconnaissance and exploitation phases. Additionally, the zone-based ACL policy blocked Administrative$\rightarrow$Critical zone traffic, providing a complementary defense layer.

\section{Case Studies}

\subsection{Case Study 1: Ransomware Propagation Attempt}

\textit{Scenario}: An attacker compromises an Administrative workstation ($h_5$) and attempts to propagate ransomware to the Critical zone EHR server ($h_1$).

\textit{Static network}: The attacker scans the network, identifies $h_1$ at a fixed IP, and delivers the ransomware payload directly. Success rate: high.

\textit{Under MTD-HealthNet}: (1) The attacker scans and identifies $h_1$'s current public IP. (2) During the preparation phase for payload delivery (estimating 60--90 seconds for exploit crafting), $h_1$'s IP rotates (120s interval). (3) The attacker's crafted payload targets the \textit{old} IP, which now maps to no host or a different host. (4) Even if the attacker rescans, the zone-based ACL policy blocks Administrative$\rightarrow$Critical traffic, providing defense-in-depth. The ransomware delivery fails due to both stale reconnaissance and policy enforcement.

\subsection{Case Study 2: Internal Lateral Movement}

\textit{Scenario}: An attacker with access to the Imaging zone ($h_3$) attempts to pivot toward other Imaging zone or Critical zone hosts to exfiltrate DICOM imaging data.

\textit{Under MTD-HealthNet}: (1) The attacker identifies $h_4$ (Imaging zone peer) at IP 172.16.0.62. (2) After 100 seconds, $h_4$'s IP rotates to 172.16.0.78. (3) The attacker's lateral movement attempt to 172.16.0.62 fails---the IP either is unassigned or now maps to a different host in a different zone. (4) The attacker must rescan, but by the time the new IP is discovered, the next rotation may have already occurred. The result is a persistent cycle of stale intelligence that significantly increases the time and effort required for successful lateral movement.

\subsection{Case Study 3: Network Scanning Disruption}

\textit{Scenario}: An external attacker performs continuous Nmap scans to build a network map of the hospital LAN.

\textit{Under MTD-HealthNet}: (1) First scan discovers 6 hosts at specific IPs. (2) Second scan (80+ seconds later) finds that Administrative zone IPs have changed; the attacker's map becomes partially invalid. (3) By the third scan, Imaging zone IPs have also rotated. (4) After 120 seconds, all zones have undergone at least one rotation. The attacker's accumulated network map contains a mixture of current and stale entries, with steady-state accuracy degrading to approximately 16.7\%. The NAT layer additionally prevents the attacker from determining the true internal network structure, as all visible IPs are from the 172.16.0.0/16 pool and reveal no information about the private 10.0.0.0/24 topology.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Strengths}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reduced predictability}: Continuous IP rotation ensures that reconnaissance data becomes stale within one hopping interval, forcing attackers to repeatedly invest scanning effort.
    \item \textbf{Dual-layer address isolation}: The private/public IP separation ensures that even if public IPs are compromised, the internal network structure remains concealed.
    \item \textbf{Zone-differentiated policy}: Risk-stratified rotation intervals accommodate the varying availability requirements of different hospital departments.
    \item \textbf{Application-layer continuity}: The transparent NAT mechanism preserves active sessions through IP transitions, validated through actual data transfers rather than ICMP tests alone.
    \item \textbf{Auditability}: Complete shuffle history stored in SQLite provides forensic traceability of all IP assignments---a requirement for HIPAA compliance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single controller dependency}: The centralized Ryu controller represents a single point of failure; controller downtime halts all MTD operations and NAT management.
    \item \textbf{Single-switch topology}: The current implementation uses a single OVS switch, which does not fully represent the multi-switch, multi-subnet topology of real hospital networks.
    \item \textbf{Legacy device compatibility}: Medical devices with hardcoded IP dependencies may require additional proxy mechanisms to function within the dynamic addressing environment.
    \item \textbf{Limited IP pool entropy}: With a pool of 50 addresses and 6 hosts, the address space entropy is $\log_2\binom{50}{6} \approx 22.7$ bits per rotation, which provides moderate but not cryptographic-strength randomness.
    \item \textbf{Synchronization overhead}: Rapid rotation intervals ($<$30 seconds) caused observable synchronization delays between the controller and host agents, limiting the practical minimum hopping interval.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Security-Performance Trade-off}

The experimental results, analyzed by Rushyendra and Jeeshitha, demonstrate a favorable trade-off: a modest 2.0\% reduction in PDR and 0.07~ms average RTT increase yield an 83.3\% reduction in reconnaissance accuracy and 80\% reduction in lateral movement success. For hospital environments where sub-second latency variations are acceptable (most healthcare applications are not latency-critical at the millisecond level), this trade-off strongly favors MTD deployment.

The zone-based approach provides an important tuning mechanism: administrators can adjust hopping intervals per zone to optimize the security-stability balance based on specific clinical requirements.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented MTD-HealthNet, developed by Rushyendra et al., a zone-based Moving Target Defense framework for hospital LANs that employs dynamic IP hopping and NAT randomization within an SDN architecture. The system continuously mutates the externally visible network topology while maintaining stable internal addressing, achieving an 83.3\% reduction in reconnaissance accuracy, a 4.7$\times$ increase in attacker effort, and an 80\% reduction in lateral movement success, with only a 2.0\% decrease in packet delivery ratio and negligible steady-state latency overhead.

The prototype demonstrates that Moving Target Defense is a viable and practical approach for enhancing hospital network security without requiring hardware upgrades or disrupting clinical operations. The zone-differentiated rotation strategy provides administrators with fine-grained control over the security-availability trade-off for different hospital departments.

\section{Future Work}

Based on the findings of this work, Jeeshitha and Siddardha identify several directions for future research:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AI-driven adaptive MTD}: Integrating machine learning models to dynamically adjust hopping intervals based on detected threat levels and network anomaly scores, replacing fixed timer-based rotation.
    \item \textbf{IDS/IPS integration}: Coupling the MTD engine with intrusion detection systems to trigger immediate IP rotation for hosts under active attack, creating a reactive defense layer complementary to the proactive rotation.
    \item \textbf{Multi-switch scalability}: Extending the architecture to multi-switch, multi-subnet topologies representative of real hospital campuses, and measuring controller scalability (CPU/memory overhead) with increasing host counts.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic port hopping}: Incorporating transport-layer port randomization alongside IP hopping, with transparent port rewriting via the SDN controller to preserve session state.
    \item \textbf{Formal security analysis}: Applying game-theoretic models to compute optimal hopping intervals as a function of attacker scanning speed and network size, providing provable security guarantees.
    \item \textbf{Real-world deployment}: Piloting MTD-HealthNet on a physical hospital testbed to validate performance under realistic traffic loads, latency constraints, and regulatory compliance requirements.
\end{itemize}
